## Design Principles

This product was designed outcomes-backward. For each tier, we started with: what does the buyer walk in with, what do they walk out with, what decisions does the output enable, and what methodology produces that output? Pricing is derived from time investment and value delivered.

Each tier produces a specific artifact the company keeps using after the engagement ends. No tier produces a document that gets read once and filed.

## Mid-Market AI Landscape (research basis for the diagnostic gap analysis)

These statistics are specific to mid-market companies ($10M-$1B revenue) and come from the RSM Middle Market AI Survey 2025, which surveyed 966 decision-makers at U.S. and Canadian mid-market firms. This is the benchmark set used in the Tier 1 gap analysis — not enterprise research adapted downward.

**Adoption is high but shallow:**
- 91% of mid-market companies use generative AI, up from 77% a year earlier (RSM 2025)
- But only 25% have fully integrated AI into core operations and workflows
- Only 37% have a well-formulated AI strategy or roadmap
- 53% feel only "somewhat prepared" to implement AI. Another 10% feel not prepared at all.

**The implementation gap is real:**
- 92% of mid-market companies using AI encountered challenges during rollout (RSM 2025)
- Top challenges: data quality, privacy/security concerns, internal skill gaps, and governance
- 39% of those who felt unprepared cited lack of in-house expertise as their top issue

**They know they need help:**
- 70% of mid-market firms using AI recognize the need for external support (RSM 2025)
- 76% have a dedicated budget for AI investments
- 47% of that budget goes to AI consulting services

**Enterprise comparison (for context, not as the primary benchmark):**
- 80%+ of enterprise AI initiatives deliver no material impact on earnings (McKinsey 2025)
- 42% of companies abandoned most AI initiatives in 2025, up from 17% prior year (S&P Global)
- 4.1x higher success rate for AI projects with sustained executive sponsorship (RAND/Deloitte/MIT)
- Only 1 in 5 companies has a mature governance model for AI (Deloitte 2026)

**What this means for the diagnostic:** The gap analysis in Tier 1 benchmarks against mid-market patterns from RSM, supplemented by enterprise research where mid-market-specific data doesn't exist. The facilitator is transparent about which benchmarks come from which source.

## Target Buyer

**Company profile:** $100M-$500M revenue, 200-2,000 employees, operationally complex (multiple functional areas), no Chief AI Officer or internal AI team.

**Primary buyer:** CEO or COO.

**Secondary buyer:** Chief Strategy Officer, CFO.

**Trigger events:** Board asked about AI strategy, competitor shipped something AI-enabled, tool licenses bought 6+ months ago with low adoption, AI pilot stalled or failed, new C-suite hire said "we're behind."

## ROI Framework

The product design needs to answer the CFO's question: what's the expected return?

**Cost avoidance (quantifiable):**
- Average mid-market company abandoned 1.1 AI initiatives in 2025 (S&P Global). Average sunk cost per abandoned project: $4.2M (Pertama Partners, sourcing RAND). Even at 10% of the enterprise figure for mid-market scale, that's $420K wasted per abandoned initiative. A $45K-$65K engagement that prevents one abandoned initiative pays for itself 6-9x.
- 92% of mid-market AI rollouts hit challenges (RSM). The operating model doesn't prevent challenges, but it creates the governance structure to catch and resolve them before they become abandonment events.

**Value creation (directional):**
- Mid-market companies with well-formulated AI strategies (37% per RSM) are able to move from pilot to production. The operating model is what turns a strategy into a production path.
- Sustained executive sponsorship creates a 4.1x improvement in AI project success rates (RAND/Deloitte/MIT). The Mandate and Pulse Check are the mechanisms that sustain sponsorship structurally, not through individual champion willpower.
- Companies that redesign workflows (not just buy tools) see the biggest EBIT impact from AI (McKinsey 2025). The Operating Sprint includes workflow integration mapping for exactly this reason.

**How to frame this for the CFO:**
"The median mid-market company will waste $200K-$500K on AI initiatives that go nowhere in the next 12 months. That's not a scare tactic — it's the math behind the 42% abandonment rate and the $4.2M average sunk cost. This engagement costs $45K-$65K and produces the operating infrastructure that determines whether your AI investments create returns or become line items on a write-off."

## Tier 1: The AI Diagnostic

### Purpose

Classify the company's AI problem and give the CEO a reusable framework for understanding where they are, where the gaps are, and what kind of intervention they actually need.

### What the buyer walks in with

A vague sense that "we should be doing more with AI" and no structured way to evaluate their current state.

### What the buyer walks out with

**The AI Diagnostic Canvas** — a one-page visual framework with three layers:

**Layer 1 — Problem Classification:** Is the primary problem strategic, organizational, or operational? Getting this right determines the intervention. A company with an operational problem doesn't need a mandate session — they need their CTO to run a better rollout.

**Layer 2 — Decision Map:** Where AI decisions are currently being made across the org, by whom, and where the gaps are. Surfaces shadow AI, uncoordinated tool purchases, informal ownership, and the decisions nobody is making.

**Layer 3 — Gap Analysis:** How the company's current state compares to mid-market success patterns from RSM, supplemented by enterprise research from McKinsey, RAND, and MIT. Benchmarked against mid-market companies specifically, not Fortune 500 patterns adapted downward. Where benchmarks are enterprise-derived, this is stated explicitly.

Plus a one-page recommendations appendix with research citations.

### What decisions this enables

- Whether to invest in organizational alignment (Tier 2) or go execute with existing resources
- Which functional areas have the most urgent gaps
- What to tell the board at the next meeting
- Whether to hire, restructure, or fix execution

### Methodology

**Before the session (your prep, 2-3 hours):**
- Research the company: org structure, recent hires, AI/data/strategy job postings (posted or pulled), tech stack if visible, recent press, competitor AI activity, public leadership statements
- LinkedIn review of leadership team
- Draft preliminary decision map hypothesis

**Before the session (their pre-work, 20-30 minutes):**
- 8-10 question pre-session questionnaire. Specific: "How many AI tools has your company purchased in the last 12 months and who approved them?" "Is there a single person accountable for AI strategy?" "Has your board asked about AI in the last two quarters?" "Have any AI pilots been launched? What happened to them?"

**The session (75-90 minutes, virtual, CEO/COO only):**

Collaborative mapping on the canvas.

- Section 1 — Problem Classification (20 min): Structured questions forcing a classification.
- Section 2 — Decision Map (30 min): Map where AI decisions are being made, by whom, and where the gaps are.
- Section 3 — Gap Analysis (20 min): Compare against mid-market success patterns. Where are they strong, where are they exposed?
- Close (10 min): Top 2-3 gaps. Preliminary recommendation.

**After the session (your work, 2-3 hours):**
- Polish canvas into finished one-page diagnostic artifact
- Add recommendations appendix with research citations
- Deliver within 48 hours

### Important design note

The diagnostic sometimes tells the company they don't need Tier 2. If the problem is operational, the right answer is "invest in your CTO's rollout plan, not more consulting." This must be genuine. The diagnostic's credibility depends on it.

## Tier 2: The Mandate

### Purpose

Get the executive team aligned on what the company will and won't do with AI, who owns it, and what success looks like — and produce the artifacts that cascade that alignment to the rest of the organization.

### What the buyer walks in with

The diagnostic canvas showing their problem classification, decision gaps, and exposure areas. A leadership team with competing opinions about AI. No shared framework for making AI decisions.

### What the buyer walks out with

**The Mandate Package** — a single integrated document with four sections, plus a board summary and communication plan:

1. **The AI Mandate** (section 1 of the integrated doc): What the company will and won't do with AI, who owns it, what success looks like at 6 and 12 months. Designed to be the first page. Printable. Pinnable to a wall.

2. **Use Case Prioritization** (section 2): The pursue/explore/kill list with rationale for each decision.

3. **Decision Rights Framework** (section 3): Who approves AI tool purchases, who evaluates vendors, who owns governance, who can kill a project. Named owners.

4. **Milestone Tracker** (section 4): 6-month or 12-month framework with named owners and specific checkpoints.

5. **Board-Ready Summary** (standalone, 2-3 pages): What the CEO presents at their next board meeting.

6. **Stakeholder Communication Plan** (standalone, 1 page): Who hears what, from whom, when, with talking points.

*Note: Items 1-4 are one integrated document with sections, not four separate PDFs. This addresses the CEO's concern about deliverable bloat. The board summary and communication plan are separate because they serve different audiences.*

### Methodology

**Before Session 1 (your prep, 4-5 hours):**
- Review diagnostic canvas
- 30-minute pre-calls with 2-3 attendees individually. Not logistics — surfacing concerns that won't come up in the group.
- Session design based on fault lines revealed in pre-calls
- Draft use case landscape for prioritization exercise

**Session 1: Alignment (half day, 4 hours, in-person)**

*Why in-person:* The mandate session requires reading body language, managing sidebar conversations, and building the kind of commitment that happens when people look each other in the eye. The skeptic who stays quiet on Zoom speaks up when they're sitting across the table. The CEO who checks email during virtual meetings is fully present in a room. The mandate document is a commitment device — it carries more weight when it was built with everyone physically present.

Who's in the room: CEO + 4-8 senior leaders.

- Block 1 — Surface (60 min): Each leader names their single biggest concern and single biggest opportunity. No debate. Map everything.
- Block 2 — Classify and Prioritize (75 min): Map AI opportunities into pursue/explore/kill. The kill list is where the real value is.
- Block 3 — Decision Rights Draft (60 min): Who approves, evaluates, governs, and kills? Force ownership onto specific people.
- Block 4 — Close and Assign (15 min): Name the open questions that need ground-truth validation before Session 2.

**Between sessions — Pressure-Testing Discovery (1 week, your work):**

*This is a key design change from v1.* Instead of sending leaders back to do homework with their teams (which produces filtered, biased data and places a burden on busy VPs), the facilitator conducts 3-5 short discovery calls (30-45 min each) directly with functional leads or team members one level below the C-suite.

What these calls accomplish:
- Unfiltered data from the people doing the work, not the leaders managing the narrative
- Ground-truth on what AI tools are actually being used, by whom, what's working, what isn't
- Early signal on which Tier 3 components will be needed (workflow complexity, risk exposure, resource gaps)
- Seeds relationships with the people who'll be in the Operating Sprint sessions

What you bring to Session 2 based on these calls:
- A draft mandate document — deliberately rough, signaling "this is a working document, bring your edits"
- A reality check on the use case prioritization from Session 1 ("your VP Ops told me the workflow integration for use case #2 is much harder than the leadership team assumed")
- Specific recommendations that are grounded in how the company actually works, not how the C-suite thinks it works

The CEO/sponsor gets the draft 48 hours before Session 2 for a gut-check.

**Session 2: Finalize (half day, 3-4 hours, in-person)**

Who's in the room: Same group as Session 1.

- Block 1 — Pressure-Test Report (45 min): You present what you learned from the between-session discovery calls. This is often the most valuable hour — leaders hear things about their own organization they didn't know.
- Block 2 — Finalize Mandate + Decision Rights (60 min): Walk through draft section by section. Debate, revise, finalize. Named owner for every section.
- Block 3 — Milestone Framework (45 min): Build the 6-month or 12-month tracker.
- Block 4 — Communication Plan (30 min): How does this cascade? Who presents what to their teams?

**After Session 2 (your work, 4-5 hours):**
- Produce the final mandate package (integrated document + board summary + communication plan)
- Deliver within one week

## Tier 3: The Operating Sprint

### Purpose

Take the mandate and build the actual operating model — the governance structure, decision frameworks, workflow integrations, and execution plan the company runs on day-to-day.

### What the buyer walks in with

The Mandate Package from Tier 2. Plus: the facilitator already has ground-truth data from the between-session discovery calls and has met the functional leads who'll participate in the sprint.

### Operating Model Components

**Core components (included in every engagement):**

1. **Governance structure** — meeting cadence, review process, escalation paths, reporting lines for AI decisions.

2. **Vendor/tool evaluation framework** — how the company evaluates, approves, and sunsets AI tools.

3. **90-day execution playbook** — week-by-week plan for Q1 of running the model. Named owners, specific deliverables, check-in cadence.

**Situational components (added based on diagnostic canvas and mandate output):**

4. **Workflow integration map** — where AI connects to existing processes, who's affected, what changes, what training is needed.

5. **Resource plan** — what roles are needed, what's hired vs. upskilled vs. outsourced, budget allocation.

6. **Risk and compliance framework** — guardrails, incident response protocol, audit readiness.

| Signal from diagnostic/mandate | Component needed |
|---|---|
| 3+ prioritized use cases with cross-functional dependencies | Workflow integration map |
| Company needs to hire, restructure, or reallocate budget | Resource plan |
| Regulated industry, sensitive data, or customer-facing AI | Risk and compliance framework |
| Simple use cases, single-function, existing team can execute | Base engagement only |

### Methodology

**Session design: 5-8 sessions over 4-6 weeks, in-person**

Two types of sessions: collaborative builds (you facilitate, they construct) and pressure-tests (you bring a draft, they refine).

**Base engagement (3 core components): 5 sessions / 4 weeks**

Session 1 — Kickoff + Governance Structure (90 min, collaborative build)
- Room: CEO/COO sponsor + all participating functional leads
- Build governance skeleton: decision cadence, reporting lines, escalation paths
- Output: Draft governance structure

Session 2 — Vendor/Tool Evaluation Framework (90 min, collaborative build)
- Room: CTO/VP Eng + VP Ops or current tool approver + procurement
- Build evaluation, approval, and sunset framework
- Output: Draft vendor evaluation framework

Session 3 — Integration + Technical Validation (90 min, pressure-test)
- Room: CTO/VP Eng + key functional leads
- You bring refined drafts from Sessions 1-2. CTO pressure-tests against technical reality.
- Output: Revised governance and vendor frameworks with technical integration notes

Session 4 — 90-Day Playbook Build (2 hours, collaborative build)
- Room: CEO/COO + CTO + 1-2 key functional leads
- Build the week-by-week execution plan for Q1
- Output: Draft 90-day playbook

Session 5 — Final Review + Handoff (2 hours, full room)
- Room: All participants + the designated operating model owner (see Handoff Protocol below)
- Present finished operating model. Each section has a named owner. Confirm adoption.
- Output: Finalized operating model

**Additional sessions for situational components:**

Workflow Integration Map: +2 sessions
- Session A (90 min, collaborative build): Functional leads + CTO/VP Eng map where use cases connect to existing workflows. *CTO is in this session from the start, not brought in later for pressure-testing.*
- Session B (90 min, pressure-test): Refined map reviewed by full group for feasibility and impact assessment.

Resource Plan: +1 session
- Session C (90 min, pressure-test): CEO/COO + CFO + HR lead. You bring draft resource plan, they pressure-test against budget and hiring realities.

Risk and Compliance Framework: +1 session (2 hours, not 90 min)
- Session D (2 hours, collaborative build): CTO + legal/compliance + CEO/COO. Build guardrails, incident response protocol, audit framework. Extended to 2 hours because governance, incident response, and audit readiness each require dedicated time, especially in regulated industries or companies handling customer data.

**Between-session work (your side):**

After each collaborative build: shape raw output into structured draft (2-3 hours).
After each pressure-test: revise based on feedback (2-3 hours).
Before final review: synthesize all components into single operating model document (5-6 hours).

There is light between-session review expected of participants: reviewing the drafts you send before pressure-test sessions (30-45 minutes per person). This is not "homework" — it's reading a document before a meeting. But it's real time and should be stated honestly.

**After final session (your work, 5-6 hours):**

Final deliverable package:
1. AI Operating Model (complete document with all selected components)
2. 90-Day Execution Playbook (week-by-week, named owners, check-in points)
3. Board Update (2-page summary for next board meeting)

Deliver within one week.

### Handoff Protocol

*This addresses the COO's question: "Who runs this on Day 1 after you leave?"*

During Session 1, the CEO/COO designates an **Operating Model Owner** — the person who will run the governance meeting, manage the vendor framework, and maintain the playbook after the engagement ends. This person attends every sprint session.

After Session 5 (the group handoff), there is a **1:1 Handoff Session (60 min)** with the Operating Model Owner. This covers:
- How to run the first governance meeting (agenda template provided)
- How to process a vendor evaluation request using the framework
- How to update the 90-day playbook as milestones are hit or missed
- How to escalate when the model breaks (because it will — the question is whether the response is structured or ad hoc)
- What triggers a call to you vs. what they can handle internally

This 1:1 is included in the base engagement price, not an add-on.

## Tier 4: The Pulse Check

### Purpose

Keep the operating model current as the AI landscape shifts. Quarterly accountability, landscape briefing, and health check that prevents the operating model from drifting.

### Two tiers

**Pulse Check (base): $5,000/quarter**

A 60-minute quarterly session focused on three things: Is the operating model working? What changed in AI that matters? Are we on track?

**Pulse Check Plus: $7,500/quarter**

Everything in the base, plus a board-ready memo each quarter and ad hoc email/Slack access between sessions.

### Methodology — Pulse Check (base)

**Before each session (your prep, 2-3 hours):**
- Review operating model and milestone tracker
- Research AI landscape changes relevant to their industry and prioritized use cases
- Prepare 3-section briefing canvas

**Before each session (their pre-work, 15 minutes):**
- 5-question pulse survey sent to CEO/COO and CTO separately.

**The session (60 minutes, virtual):**

*Pulse Checks are virtual, not in-person. The cadence is quarterly, the format is tight, and the value is in the content, not the room dynamics. This is the one tier where virtual is the right default.*

Who's in the room: CEO/COO + CTO.

- Block 1 — Operating Model Health Check (20 min): What's holding, what's drifting, where are people routing around the model?
- Block 2 — AI Landscape Briefing (15 min): 2-3 specific developments that affect their company. Connected to specific decisions.
- Block 3 — Milestone Accountability (20 min): Review prior quarter's playbook. What got done, what didn't, why. Reset targets.
- Close (5 min): Top 1-2 actions for the quarter.

**After each session (your work, 1-2 hours):**
- Update milestone tracker
- Send one-page session summary within 24 hours

### Pulse Check Plus (additions)

**Board-ready memo (1-2 hours additional prep):**
- 2-page document for the board showing operating model health, milestone progress, landscape changes, and next-quarter priorities
- Delivered 48 hours after the session

**Ad hoc access:**
- Email/Slack for operating model and AI strategy questions between quarters
- Response within 24-48 hours on business days
- Scoped to operating model questions, not unlimited advisory

### Time, Deliverables, Pricing

| Tier | Per quarter your hrs | Per quarter their hrs | Deliverables | Price |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pulse Check | 4-6 hours | 75 min | Updated tracker + session summary | $5,000/quarter |
| Pulse Check Plus | 6-9 hours | 75 min | Above + board memo + ad hoc access | $7,500/quarter |

Commitment: Quarterly. No minimum. They stay because it's working.

Format: Virtual.

## Travel Policy

Tiers 2 and 3 are in-person. The facilitator is based in the San Francisco Bay Area.

- Companies within the Bay Area: no travel surcharge
- Companies elsewhere in the US: travel expenses (flights, hotel, ground transportation) billed at cost, in addition to engagement fee
- For companies with distributed leadership teams: the engagement requires gathering the team in one location. This is the company's coordination responsibility. The facilitator travels to wherever the team convenes.

Travel is not a profit center. It's billed at cost with receipts.
